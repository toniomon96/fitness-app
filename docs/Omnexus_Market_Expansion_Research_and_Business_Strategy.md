
Omnexus Market Expansion Research and Business Strategy
Fitness industry trends shaping where the demand is going
The strongest signal in 2025–2026 is that “fitness” is increasingly being purchased as a broader health experience (strength + recovery + longevity + coaching + community), not as a single modality like cardio or class-based training. A widely circulated 2025 market update from L.E.K. Consulting and Lincoln International describes consumers redefining fitness to blend physical training with mental well-being, recovery, and lifestyle optimization; it also reports U.S. health club membership growing to ~77 million in 2024 (+6% YoY) with visits up ~8%. 
 This offers a direct strategic implication for Omnexus: your “coach + science + personalization” positioning fits the direction the market is moving, and your roadmap should emphasize that Omnexus isn’t a tracker—it’s a personalized transformation system grounded in data and behavior design.

Multiple independent trend sources converge on the same macro themes:

Strength training remains a dominant “gravity well.” L.E.K. highlights strategic gym realignment toward free weights/functional training zones and notes increasing demand for resistance training. 
 A U.S. consumer study released by the Health & Fitness Association similarly reports a shift in equipment usage patterns: in 2024, 32.1% of members trained with dumbbells/free weights (and 43.4% used treadmills), while usage of resistance machines and ellipticals declined compared with 2021. 
 This supports your decision to expand the exercise library and invest heavily in “movement patterns + swaps + technique education” rather than generic workout lists.

Recovery, longevity, and “fitness as healthcare” are moving from niche to mainstream. L.E.K. calls out growth in recovery services (cold plunges, red light, compression) and explicitly connects the market’s focus on strength/recovery to GLP‑1 adoption and broader holistic wellness demand. 
 McKinsey & Company likewise frames wellness as highly personalized and identifies weight management (including GLP‑1s), in-person fitness, and healthy aging/longevity as major growth areas. 
 For Omnexus, this means the most compelling “premium” story will combine training outcomes with recovery intelligence, adherence, and sustainable habits rather than short-term intensity.

Mobile fitness is not just large—it’s still accelerating. Sensor Tower reports global Health & Fitness app downloads hitting a new high of 3.6B in 2024 (+6% YoY), and projects the category surpassing $4B in global in‑app purchase revenue in 2025; it also notes January 2025 IAP revenue rising 10% YoY to a record $385M, underscoring the seasonal “New Year” demand spike. 
 This supports your instinct that moving to a mobile app is the next “step function” for distribution and retention.

Wearables + apps sit at the top of professional trend forecasts. The American College of Sports Medicine 2026 survey-based fitness trends list wearable technology as #1 and include both mobile exercise apps and programs for older adults in the top five. 
 This trend alignment matters because Omnexus can become the layer that turns wearable signals into training decisions and habit loops—especially for “on-the-go” demographics.

A particularly important business insight from HFA’s 2024 behavior data: coaching is increasingly valued, but users may be consuming it differently than before. HFA reports 22.6% of members worked with a personal trainer in 2024 and 32.3% did small-group training—historic highs—yet average annual session counts per participant have fallen versus 2019. 
 This is a potential opening for Omnexus: the market wants coaching and accountability, but often wants it in a lower-friction, more scalable, more affordable form.

Multi-demographic targeting without splintering the product
A core risk in “multiple markets” is building a separate product for each demographic. The opportunity is to build one platform with distinct onboarding tracks, content packaging, and distribution channels.

A strong segmentation framework is to define three consumer “contexts” plus two institutional markets, all powered by the same engine:

Gym-context consumers (commercial gyms + boutique studios).
This segment is large and growing: HFA reports 77M U.S. fitness facility members and ~96M “total customers” when including pay‑as‑you‑go and insurance/employer program users. 
 This segment cares most about: progressive overload guidance, form confidence, efficient workouts, and consistency.

A key competitive note: Planet Fitness’s own app positioning demonstrates that even HVLP gyms treat digital workouts and “gym-in-your-pocket” capabilities as part of the value proposition (including on-demand workouts, equipment tutorials, and activity tracking). 
 That signals that consumers now expect an app layer even from budget gyms, which increases the baseline expectation Omnexus must beat.

Home-context consumers (home gym, partial equipment, apartment).
This group is “constraint-driven.” Your Equipment Swap system is not just a feature; it’s your primary retention defense here. Market language is increasingly about personalization (tailored plans + data-driven progression), which L.E.K. specifically highlights as an industry tailwind alongside wearable integration and AI personalization. 

No-equipment and “micro-session” consumers (truck drivers, traveling workers, deskless professionals).
This is where your app can win by being structured, mapped to real constraints, and psychologically easy.

Truck drivers in particular are a high-need, underserved group. CDC/NIOSH notes that compared to U.S. adult workers, long-haul truck drivers have higher rates of heart disease, diabetes, hypertension, and obesity. 
 A NIOSH bulletin summarizing a national driver health survey reports ~69% of long-haul truck drivers were obese (BMI ≥30) and 17% morbidly obese, compared to ~31% and ~7% in U.S. working adults. 
 This is a strong signal that “no equipment, 10–20 minute sessions, consistency-first programming” is both socially valuable and commercially plausible—especially via employer/fleet wellness distribution.

Institutional market A: gyms and studios that don’t have a strong app.
The HFA data show coaching demand, hybrid behaviors, and increasingly diverse training modalities inside facilities. 
 Many independent operators need digital engagement to retain members, sell programs, and keep accountability between visits. Your learning system is a differentiator here: it’s “coach-the-member” content that’s traditionally expensive to deliver.

Institutional market B: employers and wellness benefits.
Even within the gym industry’s tracking, HFA explicitly includes “insurance or employer program members” as part of total penetration (31.0% “total customer penetration” in the U.S.). 
 This matters for your strategy: your product can be positioned as a “frontline-ready fitness and education system” that works for deskless workers and traveling professionals—not just office workers.

A practical way to grow across demographics without building new “features” is to build three things you already need anyway:

Onboarding segmentation (equipment + lifestyle + time budget + training age).
Program and lesson “bundles” mapped to those constraints (e.g., “5‑minute daily mobility streak,” “10‑minute strength micro-sessions,” “hotel-gym hypertrophy,” “commercial gym beginner confidence”).
Retention messaging that matches the segment’s psychology (e.g., “keep the streak” for on-the-go; “beat last week’s top set” for gym-context; “minimum effective dose” framing for busy professionals).
Gym and studio licensing and integration paths
Gym/studio licensing is attractive because it can reduce reliance on paid consumer acquisition and can monetize at the “location” or “member base” level. It also matches a well-supported industry structure: L.E.K. notes continued outperformance of high-value low-price (HVLP) and premium gym models, while mid-tier operators struggle and many shift toward specialization. 
 That bifurcation creates a “white space” for independents: they need differentiation and retention tools that do not require building an app team.

Integration reality: you don’t integrate with Planet Fitness, you integrate with the gym software layer
Large chains often have proprietary app ecosystems. What you can integrate into—at scale—is the “operating system layer” used by tens of thousands of clubs and studios: membership management, scheduling, billing, check‑in, CRM, and partner marketplaces.

The implication for Omnexus licensing is straightforward:

Build a Gym/Studio offering that can plug into the platforms independent operators already use.
Support “identity + membership status + check-in events + basic profile” synchronization so the gym views Omnexus as retention infrastructure, not a competing membership system.
Evidence that this integration route is viable:

ABC Fitness publishes an API developer portal and API reference documentation (including OAuth-secured endpoints). 
Mindbody offers a developer portal and documents integrating via its public APIs. 
Daxko’s Zen Planner exposes OpenAPI documentation for its API. 
ClassPass also runs a developer portal describing an “Inventory API” for schedules/transactions. 
This gives you a concrete pathway to build “Omnexus for Gyms” as an add-on layer rather than forcing operators to replace core systems.

Licensing packaging options that map to how gyms buy
Most gym/studio software is sold either as “per location,” “per user,” or “quote-based” bundles. Public marketplace listings show substantial variance and a tendency for vendors to quote rather than publish. For example, a Capterra listing for Exercise.com shows a starting price of $239/month. 
 Virtuagym’s published/third-party pricing references vary by source and plan structure, reinforcing that you should treat B2B pricing as a negotiated value sale rather than a single fixed sticker—especially once you include content and coaching intelligence. 

For Omnexus, you can keep this simple and still be flexible:

A Starter License aimed at single-location gyms (retention tools + learning + “programs”).
A Growth License for multi-location or high-engagement gyms (analytics + challenges + deeper integrations).
An Enterprise/Franchise License where integrations, SSO, and reporting become the value.
This is not “adding product features” so much as productizing what you already have into a B2B wrapper: branded experiences, basic admin controls, and integration connectors.

Pricing, tiering, and what $12.99 means in the real market
Your $12.99 premium price sits inside the current consumer benchmark band
Comparable consumer-facing fitness subscriptions cluster around roughly $10–$16/month in the U.S.:

Apple’s Fitness+ is $9.99/month or $79.99/year. 
Strava Premium is $11.99/month or $79.99/year (plus localized pricing in other markets). 
Fitbod lists $15.99/month and $95.99/year (when purchased on its website). 
MyFitnessPal describes annual pricing tiers such as $79.99/year for Premium and $99.99/year for Premium+ (pricing also varies by region and purchase channel). 
Peloton’s App memberships (purchased through iOS) include pricing such as $15.99/month for “App One” and higher tiers for App+. 
This strongly suggests that $12.99/month can be viable—if premium clearly means “personalized coaching + education + progress intelligence,” not just a workout list.

Cost-effectiveness depends on distribution channel and app store fees
Your margin is driven by where the subscription is purchased:

On Apple’s App Store, Apple documents that auto‑renewable subscriptions pay developers 70% during the first year and 85% after one year of paid service (minus applicable taxes). 
 Apple also offers the App Store Small Business Program with a reduced commission rate of 15% on paid apps and in‑app purchases for eligible developers (subject to program terms). 

On Google Play, Google documents service fees and reduced tiers; for example, subscriptions and the first $1M in annual earnings can qualify for lower service fees under Google’s policies. 

If you sell via the web, payment processing fees are typically much lower than app store commissions. Stripe’s published U.S. online card pricing is commonly 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction. 

What that means for your $12.99 price (illustrative math, ignoring taxes/refunds):

Apple at 70% net: ~$9.09 to you/month in year one.
Apple at 85% net: ~$11.04 to you/month after one year of paid service. 
Web via Stripe-only fees: roughly $12.99 − (2.9% + $0.30) ≈ $12.31. 
The business implication: if Omnexus leans heavily on AI compute (coach conversations, RAG retrieval, personalization) and rich media, you may want a strategy that (a) encourages annual plans for cash flow stability, and (b) offers web purchase where permissible for better margins—while staying compliant with platform policies and user experience expectations. 

Tiering that supports multiple markets without “feature sprawl”
If your priority is not more features but “everything better,” consider tiers that reflect service intensity and market channel, not random feature gating:

Free: basic library browsing + limited workouts + limited lessons (enough to prove value).
Premium (B2C): full personalization, learning paths, progression intelligence, streaks/XP, full exercise library.
Gym Partner Access (B2B2C): premium access bundled through a gym’s membership (the gym pays you a license; members get “included with membership” access).
Team/Employer Access: per-employee access bundled via employer programs.
This approach matches what you see in the market: the same core product sold through different economic buyers (consumer vs gym vs employer).

Visual and UX evolution for a true V2 “leap”
Your reference point—dramatic iOS-era design leaps—maps to a real pattern: the biggest perceived UI leaps come from a cohesive system, not from “better images.” To achieve that, you’ll want a design system that enforces consistency and modern platform-native feel.

A strong foundation is to align with platform guidelines:

Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines (HIG) provide best practices for designing great experiences across Apple platforms. 
Apple has also been promoting a “new design” direction (including material concepts like “Liquid Glass”) and provides official design resources/templates. 
Material Design 3 emphasizes accessibility-forward color systems (including dynamic color), typography hierarchy, and motion principles that make interactions feel fluid.

What “less gimmicky” usually means in practice
When founders say “too many gimmicky icons,” it typically indicates:

inconsistent icon styles (mixed line weights, filled vs outline, different metaphors),
inconsistent typography/spacing,
too many accent colors competing for attention,
celebratory animation that’s not tied to meaningful moments.
If you want an iPhone-like evolution, the most reliable pattern is:

Establish a core design language (type scale, spacing scale, color roles, icon library rules).
Implement a component library (buttons, cards, tabs, chips, lesson blocks, exercise blocks).
Create a motion language where animation is used for: state change, celebration, and guidance—not decoration. Material 3’s motion guidance is useful as a reference point for “alive but not chaotic.” 
Your existing celebration mechanics (rank-ups, streak milestones, PR confetti) are perfect “earned moments.” The UX upgrade is to make the rest of the UI calm and premium so that celebrations feel special rather than constant noise.

How to build a demo that sells the vision without building new features
Because mobile fitness downloads and spending spike around the New Year season, a great demo can materially improve conversion if launched when consumer intent is high. 

A high-performing demo package typically consists of:

a 60–90 second “hero video” showing: onboarding → personalized plan → workout guidance → streak/XP → progress timeline,
a web-based interactive walkthrough (feature tour) for investors, gyms, and partners,
3–5 short vertical clips focusing on single outcomes: “first week streak,” “equipment swap,” “coach notes,” “learning path.”
This is not “adding features.” It’s increasing perceived quality and clarity, which is often the highest ROI change you can make before scaling spend.

Mobile app strategy that supports both consumer and licensing distribution
The market data supports your move to mobile: mobile exercise apps are consistently a top trend (ACSM), and Health & Fitness app downloads are at record highs (Sensor Tower). 
 Your B2B story also improves with mobile because gyms want members engaged outside the facility.

A practical technical pathway that minimizes rebuild risk is a universal approach:

Expo documentation describes building universal apps that run on Android, iOS, and web with a single codebase, and EAS Build for store-ready binaries. 
React Native’s “New Architecture” direction is explicitly positioned as enabling higher-quality experiences at scale, and recent releases emphasize this milestone. 
This matters because your V2 “visual leap” will be easier if you can unify UI primitives and motion across platforms rather than fragmenting.

Two operational realities you should budget for early:

App Store review requires compliance with Apple’s guidelines across UI, functionality, and store behavior. 
Subscription economics differ across channels (Apple/Google fees vs web fees), and these economics influence whether you push annual plans, bundles, or licensing. 
AI-native development, DevOps, and SRE upgrades for faster product quality
You asked specifically about “agentic AI,” orchestrators, and improving DevOps/SRE. The state of tooling in 2025–2026 has shifted from “code completion” to “agents that can own tasks,” but it only works well if you build the right workflow and observability foundation.

Agentic coding in practice
GitHub has formally announced Copilot “agent mode” and a coding agent workflow that can implement tasks/issues and submit pull requests. 
 This is important because it enables a new engineering loop:

You write a spec (or refine one).
Assign the issue to an agent.
The agent writes code and runs tests in CI.
You review like a senior engineer, focusing on architecture and correctness.
For “quality upgrades” (polish, refactors, content pipelines, UI consistency), this workflow is especially effective—because many tasks are well-scoped and testable.

On the Claude side, Claude Code documentation highlights connecting tools via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), positioning it as a way for agents to read design docs and interact with external systems. 
 Anthropic’s MCP announcement frames it as an open standard enabling secure two-way connections between data sources and AI tools. 

The operational takeaway: when you build your product and company systems with MCP-style tool boundaries, agents can help with:

code generation + refactor,
documentation updates,
test generation,
“migration scripts + data validation,”
release notes and changelogs.
DevOps and observability foundation
If you want to ship quickly without losing reliability, you need visibility into behavior changes and regressions—especially with personalization, learning streaks, and XP accounting.

OpenTelemetry is positioned as a vendor-neutral framework for generating and exporting telemetry (traces, metrics, logs). 
 This aligns with your needs because it lets you instrument:

“workout flow latency,”
“lesson completion drop-off,”
“XP write failures,”
“streak freeze usage anomalies,”
“search latency and error rates,” without being locked into a single tool ecosystem.
The key is to treat product analytics and reliability telemetry as one integrated system: the same “event log” mindset you’re already using for XP (xp_events) should exist for operational signals too.

How this ties back to “make everything better, not add features”
Agentic development plus strong observability is how you “raise the floor” across the entire product:

faster iteration on lesson content quality,
tighter UI consistency through component refactors,
better performance and fewer bugs through instrumentation,
safer experimentation on pricing/tiering and onboarding flows.
Combined with the market trend signals above—strength focus, holistic wellness, mobile acceleration, and demand for scalable coaching—this is a credible path to building a consumer product that can also be licensed as infrastructure to gyms and employers.
